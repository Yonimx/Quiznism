    Electron configurations - A summary of the positions of the electrons around a nucleus.

    The electron configuration of an element describes how the electrons are distributed throughout its atomic orbitals.

    All atomic subshells that contain electrons are ordered in a sequence according to a standard nomenclature, with the number of electrons each subshell has indicated in superscript.

    The usual nomenclature usually results in long electron configurations, particularly for elements with a very high atomic number.

    The standard notation can be replaced with a shortened or simplified version.

    The sequence of fully filled subshells that represent the electronic configuration of a noble gas are replaced in the abbreviated notation by the symbol for that noble gas enclosed in square brackets.


    After Ernest Rutherford and Niels Bohr's Bohr model of the atom was introduced in 1913, this terminology for the distribution of electrons in atomic orbitals of atoms was put into use.

    It is ised for:

    • Finding out an element's valency
    • Predicting the properties of a group of elements (elements with related electron configurations typically have related properties).
    • Interpreting atomic spectra.
